### H2: What Tools Can You Use to Cut PVC Conduit?
**Purpose:** Directly answer the core question with tool options and guidance
**Key points:**
- Ratcheting PVC cutter: Best for 2" and under, clean cuts, $10-20, but limit to 10 cuts due to hand fatigue
- Hacksaw with 18-24 TPI blade: Versatile, any diameter, requires deburring
- Miter saw: For large quantities, precision cuts, overkill for most DIY projects
- Copper tubing cutter: Special use - cutting conduit with wires inside
**Golden Insight:** Hand fatigue limit of 10 cuts with ratcheting cutter
**Opinion:** Recommend ratcheting cutter for most DIY projects under 10 cuts

### H3: Which Tool Should You Choose?
**Purpose:** Decision guidance based on project size
**Key points:**
- Under 10 cuts, 2" or smaller: Ratcheting PVC cutter
- Large project or varied diameters: Hacksaw with fine-tooth blade
- Professional/production work: Miter saw

### H2: How Do You Cut PVC Conduit Step by Step?
**Purpose:** Step-by-step tutorial (inverted pyramid - main steps first)
**Structure:** Numbered list with brief explanations
**Steps:**
1. Measure and mark (use tape for straight line guide)
2. Secure the conduit (V-blocks, vise, or foot)
3. Make the cut (technique varies by tool)
4. Deburr both edges (critical step - detailed in next section)
**Opinion:** Always secure the conduit - freehand cuts lead to crooked results

### H2: Why Is Deburring Critical for PVC Conduit?
**Purpose:** Address the often-skipped step with conduit-specific importance
**Key points:**
- Sharp edges nick wire insulation during pulling
- Damaged insulation can cause arcs, sparks, or shorts
- Both inside and outside edges need attention
- Tools: Deburring tool, round file, utility knife, pliers
**Golden Insight:** Tactile quality check - "Run your fingers along the reamed edges"
**Opinion:** Never skip deburring for conduit - it's not cosmetic, it's a safety requirement

### H2: Can You Cut PVC Conduit in Cold Weather?
**Purpose:** Cover the temperature consideration competitors miss
**Key points:**
- PVC becomes brittle below 50F, very brittle below 20F
- Dull blades + cold PVC = cracking
- Ratcheting cutters not recommended in cold (compression can crack)
- Use fine-tooth saw (16-18 TPI) in cold conditions
- Pre-warm pipes to room temperature if possible
**Golden Insight:** 50F temperature threshold for safe cutting
**Warning:** After cutting in cold weather, inspect for cracks; recut 2" beyond any visible damage
**Opinion:** If working in cold weather, switch from ratcheting cutter to hacksaw

### H2: How Do You Cut Conduit With Wires Already Inside?
**Purpose:** Address unique real-world scenario competitors ignore
**Key points:**
- Copper tubing cutter method: Score and rotate, won't damage wires
- Score and snap for 1-1/2" and under with ratcheting cutter
- Mason's line method: Works for PVC but WARNING - damages THHN wire insulation
- Protective sleeve method for larger conduit
**Golden Insight:** Copper tubing cutter "cut cleanly through without any damage to the wires"
**Warning Box:** Mason's string WILL cut through wire sheathing - avoid this method with wires inside
**Opinion:** Copper tubing cutter is the safest method when wires are already installed

### H2: What Are Common Mistakes to Avoid?
**Purpose:** Troubleshooting and prevention
**Key points:**
- Using dull blades (causes cracking and jagged cuts)
- Skipping deburring (leads to wire damage)
- Cutting in cold without precautions
- Not securing the conduit (crooked cuts)
- Using wood saws (large teeth chip PVC)
- Applying too much hacksaw pressure (causes cracking)
**Opinion:** Most failures come from dull tools and unsecured conduit - address these first
