### H2: What Makes Mayonnaise Different from Other Viscous Products?

**Purpose:** Establish the product-first framework - why mayonnaise requires special consideration

**Key Points:**
- Viscosity range: 3000-4500 cP (higher than many sauces)
- Thixotropic behavior: viscosity decreases under shear, recovers at rest
- Yield stress variation: Traditional mayo 360 Pa vs low-fat 30 Pa (12x difference)
- Emulsion fragility: oil-in-water structure breaks under excessive shear
- Oxygen sensitivity: lipid oxidation causes rancidity

**Opinion:** Don't treat mayonnaise as "just another viscous product" - its emulsion structure demands specific handling that generic viscous-product equipment often can't provide.

### H2: Which Filling System Protects Mayonnaise's Emulsion Structure?

**Purpose:** Directly answer the core question about filling system selection

**Key Points:**
- Piston fillers: +/-0.2% to +/-0.5% accuracy, but require cylinder grease (hygiene concern)
- Rotary lobe pumps: ~0.4% accuracy, non-pulsating flow, 3-A compliant, gentler on product
- Key specification: shear rate below 100 s-1 threshold
- Large pump clearances + slow operation = product protection

**Opinion:** For food-grade applications, rotary lobe pumps often outperform piston fillers despite slightly lower accuracy specs. The hygiene advantages and gentler handling protect both product quality and food safety.

### H2: How Does Temperature Affect Mayonnaise Filling Performance?

**Purpose:** Address temperature pathway control (competitor gap)

**Key Points:**
- Temperature affects viscosity: warmer = thinner = different fill behavior
- Constant temperature along entire pathway required (tank, hoses, nozzles)
- If heating not possible: PLC can adjust fill time/pump torque dynamically
- Cold-start issues vs. mid-shift temperature drift

**Opinion:** Temperature control is non-negotiable for consistent fill weights. Machines without pathway heating force operators into constant recalibration.

### H2: What Features Prevent Air Incorporation During Filling?

**Purpose:** Address air/aeration prevention (major competitor gap)

**Key Points:**
- Air bubbles cause: inconsistent fill weights, shelf life reduction, visual defects
- Vacuum/de-aeration systems before filling
- Anti-drip features: anti-drip valves, bottom-up filling, end-of-cycle suck-back
- Nitrogen flushing for headspace management

**Opinion:** Shelf life extension of 20-30% is achievable with proper air-free filling and nitrogen flushing - often more effective than adding antioxidants.

**Internal link opportunity:** nitrogen flushing

### H2: Which Packaging Format Works Best for Mayonnaise?

**Purpose:** Compare packaging formats with mayonnaise-specific considerations

**Key Points:**
- Glass jars: best oxygen barrier, wide mouth for viscous dispensing, but heavy/breakable
- PET bottles: 20% weight savings vs glass, compatible with existing lines
- Pouches: 58% less fossil fuel, maximum product evacuation, requires different line
- Bag-in-box: institutional/bulk, extended shelf life
- Tubes: portion control for foodservice

**Opinion:** Unless oxygen barrier is your primary concern, PET with oxygen scavenger technology now matches glass performance at lower weight and cost.

**Internal link opportunity:** sauce packaging, form fill seal machines

### H2: What Sanitary Design Standards Apply to Mayonnaise Packaging?

**Purpose:** Address CIP requirements (competitor gap)

**Key Points:**
- Mayonnaise = protein + fat = more robust CIP cycles required
- 3-A standards compliance for dairy-adjacent products
- Surface roughness below 0.8 um for food-grade contact
- Mix-proof valve installation prevents cross-contamination

**Opinion:** Don't underestimate CIP requirements. Mayonnaise's protein and fat content means cleaning cycles that work for water-based products will fail here.
