### Main Findings

**Round 1 - Foundation & Technical:**

1. **Temperature Limit Mechanism (Glass Transition)**
   - Rigid PVC has a glass transition temperature (Tg) of 80°C (176°F)
   - Below Tg: polymer chains are frozen in rigid state
   - Above Tg: chains have increased mobility, material softens
   - Maximum operating temperature (140°F) is well below Tg as safety margin
   - "Even approaching the Tg can be critical and lead to PVC failure"

2. **Continuous vs Intermittent Temperature Limits**
   - Continuous operation: 140°F (60°C) max
   - With pressure: 100°F (38°C) recommended max
   - Short-time/intermittent: up to 95°C (203°F) possible
   - Drainage systems: up to 100°C for less than 2 minutes intermittent
   - "The de-rating factors assume sustained elevated service temperatures"

3. **Temperature De-Rating Table (Complete)**
   | Temperature | De-rating Factor |
   |-------------|------------------|
   | 73°F (23°C) | 1.00 (baseline) |
   | 80°F (27°C) | 0.88 |
   | 90°F (32°C) | 0.75 |
   | 100°F (38°C) | 0.62 |
   | 110°F (43°C) | 0.50 |
   | 120°F (49°C) | 0.40 |
   | 130°F (54°C) | 0.30 |
   | 140°F (60°C) | 0.20-0.22 |

**Round 2 - Data & Evidence:**

4. **CPVC as High-Temperature Alternative**
   - CPVC max temperature: 200°F (93°C)
   - Chlorine content: 63-69% vs PVC's 57%
   - Higher chlorine = stronger molecular chain bonding
   - Price premium: 25-30% more expensive than PVC
   - "The higher chlorine content in CPVC increases the binding energy between molecular chains"

5. **Plasticizer Effects on Temperature**
   - Plasticizers lower the glass transition temperature
   - Rigid PVC Tg: ~80°C
   - Flexible PVC Tg: can go as low as -42°C with efficient plasticizers
   - "Depending on the level of plasticizer, Tg reduces below room temperature, making PVC elastomeric"
   - DINP: better thermal stability than DEHP
   - TOTM: best for high-temperature applications (automotive, electrical cables)

6. **Heat Resistance Improvement Methods**
   - Heat-resistant modifiers: acrylates and maleimides
   - Calcium-zinc stabilizers for medical-grade PVC
   - Up to 15% by weight thermal stabilizers in rigid PVC
   - "These additives can increase the glass transition temperature of PVC and enhance the dimensional stability at high temperatures"

**Round 3 - User Perspectives & Real-World Issues:**

7. **Cold Temperature Behavior**
   - PVC becomes brittle below 0°C (32°F)
   - At 32°F: still maintains 70-90% of room-temperature strength
   - Glass transition explains brittleness: molecular motion slows
   - Special plasticizers (DOA, DOS) can reduce low-temperature brittleness
   - "Fifty years of field experience confirm that PVC pipe more than measures up to the rigors of cold-weather installation"

8. **Thermal Expansion Considerations**
   - Coefficient: 2.9 x 10^-5 in/in/°F
   - Rule: 3.6 inches expansion per 100°F change per 100 ft pipe
   - Plastics expand 4-5x more than metal pipes
   - Expansion loops needed if temperature variation exceeds 25°F
   - 80 ft PVC run, 73°F to 120°F = 1.35 inches expansion

9. **Real-World Failure Cases**
   - Flue pipe failure: temperatures far exceeded 140°F limit, caused carbon monoxide and mold damage
   - Vinyl siding warping: reflective windows concentrated heat, causing softening near Tg
   - Solar water heating: black-painted PVC expanded so much walls became paper-thin, blew off
   - Freezing: bulging and multiple crack initiation sites at subfreezing temperatures
   - Thermal expansion leaks: account for 23% of CPVC system failures

10. **Code and Installation Requirements**
    - Many codes prohibit plastic pipes within 24-48 inches of water heaters
    - PVC prohibited by most codes for residential water distribution inside buildings
    - Recommended: use stainless steel flex hoses near water heaters
    - "At the maximum operating temperature for PVC (140°F), the strength is derated to approximately 20%"

## Insight Synthesis

### Golden Insights

| # | Insight | Source | Suggested Use |
|---|---------|--------|---------------|
| 1 | At 140°F, PVC retains only 20% of its room-temperature pressure capacity - not just "reduced" | Multiple manufacturer sources | Hook or section opener - surprising how dramatic the reduction is |
| 2 | Plastics expand 4-5x more than metal pipes - many installations fail because designers don't account for this | Charlotte Pipe | Installation guidance section - practical "gotcha" |
| 3 | 23% of CPVC system failures are caused by thermal expansion - not the material itself, but poor installation | Industry statistics | Counter-intuitive finding - material is fine, installation is the issue |
| 4 | Rigid PVC Tg is 80°C but flexible PVC Tg can go as low as -42°C - same base material, totally different behavior | Research sources | Explaining plasticizer effect - dramatic comparison |

### Counter-Intuitive Findings

1. **Common assumption:** PVC pipes fail because they can't handle the temperature
   **Research shows:** Many failures are actually thermal expansion issues (23% of CPVC failures), not the temperature limits themselves

2. **Common assumption:** Cold weather is a major problem for PVC
   **Research shows:** "Fifty years of field experience confirm that PVC pipe more than measures up to the rigors of cold-weather installation" - still retains 70-90% strength at 32°F

### Quotable Voices

1. **Who:** Charlotte Pipe training manager
   **Quote:** "Plastics will expand and contract four to five times more than metallic systems"

2. **Who:** Madison Group (failure analysis experts)
   **Quote:** "Even approaching the Tg can be critical and lead to PVC failure"

### Proposed Core Thesis

**PROPOSED THESIS:** Understanding the "why" behind PVC temperature limits (glass transition temperature) helps you choose the right material and avoid the most common installation mistakes.

**Supported by:**
- Rigid PVC's Tg of 80°C explains the 140°F max operating limit (safety margin)
- Plasticizers can dramatically shift this limit (from 80°C down to -42°C)
- Most failures aren't from exceeding temperature - they're from ignoring thermal expansion
- CPVC exists specifically for applications above PVC's limits
