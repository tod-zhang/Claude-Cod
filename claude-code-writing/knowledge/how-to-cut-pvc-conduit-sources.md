### Main Findings

**Round 1 - Foundation & Technical:**

**Cutting Tools Overview:**
- **Ratcheting PVC Cutter:** Best for pipes up to 2" diameter. Clean cuts, minimal burrs, $10-20 price range. Limited by hand fatigue after 10+ cuts.
- **Hacksaw:** Versatile, works on any diameter, most people already own one. Requires 18-24 TPI blade. Leaves burrs requiring cleanup.
- **Miter Saw:** Best for large quantities or precise 90-degree cuts. Fast and accurate but expensive and not portable. Requires clamping the pipe.
- **String/Wire Saw:** For tight spaces or cutting with wires inside. Loop around pipe and saw back-and-forth.

**PVC Conduit vs PVC Pipe - Key Differences:**
- Conduit is gray, pipe is white
- Conduit has thinner walls (not pressure-rated)
- Conduit has UV resistance for outdoor use
- Conduit has fire-retardant properties
- Conduit fittings have gradual bends and no ridges (for wire pulling)
- Conduit interior must be smooth to prevent wire damage

**Round 3 - User Perspectives & Alternatives:**

**Common Mistakes (from forums):**
1. Using dull blades - causes cracking and jagged cuts
2. Skipping deburring - leads to wire damage during pulling
3. Cutting in cold weather without precautions - causes brittle fractures
4. Not securing the pipe - results in crooked cuts
5. Using wood saws - large teeth chip the PVC
6. Applying too much pressure with hacksaw - causes cracking

**Pro Tips from Practitioners:**
- "Use painters tape or electrical tape around the circumference for a perfectly straight guide line"
- "For large pipes, wrap paper around the pipe - when edges align, you have a square mark"
- "Run your fingers along the reamed edges to remove little bits of plastic" (tactile quality check)
- "After cutting in cold weather, check the end for potential new cracks. If there are any, recut the pipe two inches beyond the visible end"

**Round 4 - Differentiation Deep Dive:**

**Cutting Conduit with Wires Inside (unique scenario):**
1. **Copper Tubing Cutter Method:** "After only a few rotations, it cut cleanly through without any damage to the wires. The inside edge of the pipe had a bit of a ridge, but that was quickly removed with a scrape of a flat-blade screwdriver." (Fine Homebuilding, Issue #322)
2. **Score and Snap Method:** "For anything under 1-1/2", a ratcheting PVC cutter can be used to deeply score the PVC and then just snap it."
3. **Mason's Line Method:** Friction cuts through PVC quickly, but WARNING - "Mason's string WILL cut through the sheathing of most cables. THHN wire can't hold up to a nylon string saw."
4. **Protective Sleeve Method:** For larger conduit, slide a smaller conduit over the wires inside to protect them while cutting with a reciprocating saw.

**Cold Weather Cutting (key differentiator):**
- PVC becomes brittle below 50F, very brittle below 20F
- Pre-warm pipes to room temperature before cutting
- Ratchet cutters NOT recommended in cold - compression can crack pipe
- Use fine-tooth saw blades (16-18 TPI) in cold weather
- Inspect cut ends for cracks after cutting in cold conditions
- Schedule 80 withstands cold better than Schedule 40

**Schedule 40 vs 80 Cutting Differences:**
- Schedule 40: Easier to cut, lighter weight, standard reaming
- Schedule 80: Harder to cut (thicker walls), may require additional beveling of interior edge
- Same outside diameter means fittings interchange
- Schedule 80 has smaller inside diameter due to thicker walls

**Why Deburring is Critical for Conduit:**
- Sharp edges can nick wire insulation during pulling
- Damaged insulation can cause arcs, sparks, or shorts
- "As conductors are pulled through the conduit, they will rub against the inside walls of the bend and potentially damage the wires"
- Use deburring tool, round file, utility knife, or even pair of pliers
- "Run your fingers along the reamed edges" as quality check

### Counter-Intuitive Findings

- **Common assumption:** Any saw or cutter works fine for PVC conduit
- **Research shows:** Ratchet cutters can crack cold PVC, mason's line can damage wires, and wood saws chip the material

- **Common assumption:** Deburring is just for appearance
- **Research shows:** Deburring is critical for conduit because sharp edges damage wire insulation during pulling, potentially causing electrical hazards

### Quotable Voices

- **Fine Homebuilding contributor (Addison Oliveira):** Copper tubing cutter "cut cleanly through without any damage to the wires"

### Proposed Core Thesis

**PROPOSED THESIS:** Cutting PVC conduit correctly is about more than just making a cut - it's about protecting the wires that will run through it. The right tool, proper temperature conditions, and thorough deburring make the difference between a professional installation and a potential safety hazard.

**Supported by:**
- Deburring prevents wire insulation damage during pulling
- Temperature affects PVC brittleness and cutting quality
- Different scenarios (wires inside, cold weather) require different approaches
- Tool selection depends on project size and conduit diameter
