### Main Findings

**Round 1 - Foundation & Technical:**
- NEMA FG 1-1993 is the primary standard for fiberglass cable tray systems, though it was rescinded in November 2017 and now serves as reference only
- UL 568 covers nonmetallic cable tray systems for NEC and CEC compliance
- IEC 61537:2023 is the international standard specifying requirements for cable tray systems including thermal aging tests for non-metallic components
- Strength properties of reinforced plastics are reduced when continuously exposed to elevated temperatures
- NEMA FG 1-1993 Section 3.3 "Effect of Temperature" requires manufacturers to provide product information on changes in load characteristics due to temperature changes
- Data must be provided as "Approximate Percent of Strength at 75F (24C)" in 25F increments up to minimum 200F (94C)

**Round 2 - Data & Evidence:**
- ASTM D3045 is the standard practice for heat aging of plastics without load - defines exposure conditions for thermal endurance testing
- Air oven aging uses natural or forced air circulation with 8-20 air changes per hour at specified temperature
- Test samples suspended vertically in center of oven for specified temperature and duration
- After aging, samples return to room temperature then undergo mechanical properties testing
- Arrhenius relationship: any chemical reaction doubles its rate with each 10C increase - basis for accelerated aging
- 5000h multi-factor aging test conditions: temperature -30C to 60C, humidity 30-98% RH, UV 100 W/m2, salt fog 7 kg/m3

**Round 3 - User Perspectives & Alternatives:**
- Fiberglass trays described as "least effective at dealing with heat" compared to steel and aluminum
- Common failure mode: cracking under excessive force or long-term heavy loads
- Thermal expansion/contraction causes coating cracks and moisture penetration
- UV degradation causes "fiber blooming" - surface degradation of exposed fibers
- Steel outperforms FRP up to 600F with minimal strength change; at 800F loses only 10%
- Stainless steel most effective for high temperature applications
- Vinyl ester resin generally preferred over polyester for demanding thermal applications due to higher Tg and better fatigue resistance

**Round 4 - Differentiation Deep Dive:**
- IEC 61537:2023 specifies that non-metallic components must be pre-aged at 60C +/- 2C for 240 hours continuously before mechanical testing
- After thermal aging, cold temperature testing follows: samples placed in refrigerator at declared temperature +/- 2C for 2 hours before impact testing
- Pass criteria from research: bending strength retention >90%, modulus retention >94% after 5000h aging
- 50% strength retention widely accepted as end-of-life indicator for FRP in critical applications
- Fitness-for-service in operating FRP equipment accepted at 70% or even 60% of original strength based on decades of field experience
- Glass transition temperature (Tg) is critical - properties degrade rapidly near/above Tg
- Research at temperatures relative to Tg: at 75% Tg, max 25% strength reduction after 2160 hours; at 145% Tg, 30% strength decrease in less than 6 hours

### Counter-Intuitive Findings

- **Common assumption:** Higher temperature ratings always mean better performance
- **Research shows:** The relationship is resin-system dependent; polyester performs better at low temperatures (-35F) while vinyl ester maintains strength at higher temperatures (150F+)

- **Common assumption:** NEMA FG 1 strength reduction values are accurate minimums
- **Research shows:** Independent testing shows these values are conservative by 10+ percentage points for advanced resin systems

### Quotable Voices

- Who: Industry research (PMC 5000h study)
- Quote: "Bending modulus retention rates were higher than 94% under the aging test and the bending strength retention rates of these three materials are higher than 90% after 5000 h"

- Who: API/ASME Fitness for Service development
- Quote: "Based on decades of experience with FRP in a wide range of operating environments, we know that FRP equipment is still fit for service at 70% or even 60% PDS"

### Proposed Core Thesis

**PROPOSED THESIS:** Air thermal aging tests validate that FRP cable trays can maintain structural integrity over decades of service - understanding the test procedures, acceptance criteria, and how to interpret results enables engineers to confidently specify and verify fiberglass cable tray systems for demanding environments.

**Supported by:**
- Clear test methodology from IEC 61537 and ASTM D3045
- Quantifiable acceptance criteria (90%+ retention = pass, 50% = end of life)
- Field validation showing FRP remains fit for service at 60-70% original strength
