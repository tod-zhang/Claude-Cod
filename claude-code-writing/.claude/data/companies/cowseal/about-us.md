## Part 2: Target User Analysis

### User Type 1: The Complete Beginner

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Industry** | New maintenance personnel, facility managers without mechanical background, small business owners with pump equipment |
| **Knowledge Level** | Novice (0-6 months exposure) |
| **Goals** | Understand what mechanical seals do; identify when seals need replacement; make basic purchasing decisions |

**What They Already Know**:
- Pumps move liquids
- Pumps sometimes leak
- Parts need to be replaced occasionally
- Basic awareness that seals exist

**What They Need to Learn**:
- What a mechanical seal is and how it works
- Why mechanical seals are important for pump operation
- Signs that indicate seal failure
- Basic seal terminology (faces, springs, O-rings)
- How to identify the right replacement seal

**Questions They Search For**:
- "What is a mechanical seal?"
- "Why is my pump leaking?"
- "Mechanical seal basics"
- "How do pump seals work?"
- "Pump seal replacement guide"
- "Mechanical seal vs packing difference"

**Typical Roles/Titles**:
- New maintenance staff at water treatment facilities
- Small business owners with pump equipment
- Facility managers without mechanical background
- Junior pump operators

**Pain Points & Challenges**:
- Confusion about why pumps leak and how to prevent it
- Difficulty understanding technical documentation from seal suppliers
- Uncertainty about when to replace seals vs. repair pumps
- Fear of making wrong purchasing decisions due to lack of knowledge

**Content Preferences**:

| Aspect | Preference |
|--------|-----------|
| **Reading Time** | 5-8 minutes |
| **Device** | Mobile-first (60%+ mobile) |
| **Content Length** | 800-1200 words |
| **Format** | Short paragraphs, bullet points, diagrams |

**Decision Factors**:
- Clear explanation of what seals do and why they matter
- Visual aids and simple diagrams
- Step-by-step guidance without overwhelming technical details
- Confidence that they can identify basic seal problems

**Writing Approach**:

| Element | Recommendation |
|---------|----------------|
| **Tone** | Friendly, encouraging, patient |
| **Style** | Conversational; use analogies and real-world examples |
| **Complexity** | Simple sentences; define all jargon; avoid acronyms or explain them immediately |
| **Structure** | Start with "why it matters"; build concepts step-by-step |
| **Word Choice** | Everyday language; assume no prior knowledge |

**Audience-Specific Guidelines**:

✓ Use everyday analogies: "Think of a mechanical seal like the lid on a pressure cooker..."
✓ Celebrate understanding: "You now know the most important part."
✓ Anticipate confusion: "This is where many people get confused, so let's slow down."
✓ Provide visual cues: "Picture two flat rings pressed together..."

✗ Assume any prior knowledge about seals or pumps
✗ Use unexplained acronyms: Never write "Check the API plan" without explaining
✗ Chain multiple concepts together

### Product Mention Guidelines

| Guideline | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| **Max Mentions** | 1-2 product references per article (solution-focused) |
| **Style** | Educational, not promotional. Product is THE SOLUTION, not the hero |
| **Placement** | Within relevant technical discussion, not intro/conclusion |
| **Tone** | "This can be addressed by..." NOT "Our product is..." |
| **Linking** | Link to product page only if genuinely helpful for reader |

**What Makes a Good Product Mention:**
- ✅ Appears where reader is looking for a solution
- ✅ Phrased as industry solution, not company advertisement
- ✅ Provides technical value (specs, capabilities)
- ✅ Reader would find it helpful even without buying

**What Makes a Bad Product Mention:**
- ❌ Feels inserted/forced into unrelated content
- ❌ Uses promotional language ("best", "leading", "superior")
- ❌ Appears in intro or conclusion (too promotional)
- ❌ Multiple mentions of same product
